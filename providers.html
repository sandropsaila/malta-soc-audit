What is a SOC Audit Provider?

A SOC audit provider is a licensed assurance firm — typically a CPA firm or accredited auditor — that independently examines and reports on a service organisation's internal controls. They are distinct from the companies that receive the audit (listed in the main registry). To issue a SOC 2 or ISAE 3402 report, a firm must hold appropriate professional licences (e.g. ACCA, CPA Malta, or IAASB membership) and comply with independence requirements.

In Malta, SOC audit engagements are typically conducted by the local offices of the Big-4 networks (Deloitte, KPMG, EY, PwC) and mid-tier firms (RSM, BDO, Grant Thornton, Contact Advisory Services) and MDIA-specialist firms (STIS Group, Fact Technologies), all of which are regulated by the Malta Accountancy Board (MAB) and the Accountancy Profession Act. Some engagements are also conducted by the parent offices of these networks on behalf of Malta-licensed entities.

When selecting a provider, organisations should verify the firm's independence from the auditee, their experience with the relevant Trust Services Criteria (for SOC 2) or ISAE standards, and their familiarity with Malta-specific regulatory requirements (MGA, MFSA, FIAU).

How to Choose a SOC Provider

1. Verify independence. The auditing firm must be independent of the service organisation — it cannot provide both internal audit/consulting and external SOC attestation services to the same client within the same period (IAASB independence rules).

2. Check Malta regulatory familiarity. For MFSA-licensed investment firms and payment institutions, choose a provider familiar with the MFSA's ICT governance guidance, DORA obligations, and FIAU AML reporting — these directly affect the scope of system certification reports.

3. Match firm size to your scale. Big-4 firms offer global methodology and multi-jurisdiction reach; mid-tier firms (RSM, BDO, Grant Thornton, Contact Advisory Services) and MDIA-specialist firms (STIS Group, Fact Technologies) often provide more competitive pricing and dedicated partner attention for SMEs and scale-ups.

4. Clarify Type I vs Type II. Type I reports (design only, point-in-time) are faster and cheaper but carry less weight with sophisticated clients. Type II reports (operating effectiveness over 6–12 months) are required by most enterprise contracts and regulatory frameworks.

5. Consider readiness first. All providers listed offer readiness or GAP assessments before formal attestation — these are strongly recommended to avoid scope surprises during the audit itself.
